# Module 1, Lesson 1: What University Really Costs

> **Module:** Financing Education
> **Lesson:** 1 of 10
> **Reading time:** ~4 minutes
> **Audience:** Ages 15–18, international school students

---

## The Number Nobody Tells You

Ask most students how much university costs, and they will quote the tuition fee. Ask their parents, and they will probably do the same.

Tuition is the price on the website. It is not the cost of going.

This lesson introduces a more honest framework: **Total Cost of Attendance (TCA)**. TCA is the real, complete amount that leaves your family's account each year you are at university. It includes tuition — but it includes a lot more besides.

Understanding TCA is not about scaring you off university. It is about going in with your eyes open, so that when you make one of the biggest financial decisions of your life, you are working from the actual number, not an optimistic partial one.

---

## Why the Gap Between Tuition and TCA Matters

Here is a simple example. Imagine two students, both attending the same university with the same tuition fee of $20,000 per year.

Student A lives on campus, uses the meal plan, takes taxis between classes, and buys all required textbooks new. Her real annual cost: closer to $36,000.

Student B shares a flat with three others, cooks most meals, uses public transport, and borrows textbooks from the library. His real annual cost: closer to $26,000.

Same tuition. $10,000 difference in what university actually costs them. Multiply that across four years, and the gap is $40,000 — enough to matter enormously when they graduate.

This is why TCA thinking exists. Tuition is fixed; almost everything else is not.

---

## The Six Categories That Make Up TCA

Every university student's annual costs fall into six buckets. You will spend the next few lessons learning each one in detail. For now, the important thing is to know they all exist.

1. **Tuition fees** — the headline number, paid to the university
2. **Accommodation** — rent for wherever you live
3. **Food** — meal plans, groceries, or both
4. **Transport** — getting to campus, travelling home, and everything in between
5. **Course materials** — textbooks, software, equipment, printing
6. **Personal spending** — phone, clothing, socialising, subscriptions, everything else

Together, these six categories are your TCA. In Lesson 2, you will learn how to estimate each one and build your own personal figure. In Lesson 3, you will see how TCA changes dramatically depending on which country you study in.

---

## One More Thing: The Time Multiplier

Degrees take longer than advertised more often than they finish on schedule. Programme switches, retaken modules, gap semesters, visa delays — any of these stretch the timeline. Every extra semester multiplies all six cost categories.

A three-year degree that takes four years does not cost 33% more. It costs 33% more tuition, plus an extra year of rent, food, transport, and personal spending. The real additional cost is typically 40–50% above the original plan.

When you estimate TCA, always ask: what does this look like if it takes a year longer?

---

## Key Takeaways

- Tuition is one component of university cost, not the total
- **Total Cost of Attendance (TCA)** covers all six spending categories across your full degree
- The gap between tuition and TCA is often large, and is partly within your control
- Degrees frequently run longer than planned — build that possibility into any cost estimate

---

## Reflection Question

Think about one university you are considering. Beyond tuition, what other costs can you name? Which ones do you think would be hardest to control?
