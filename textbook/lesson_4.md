# Module 1, Lesson 4: Hidden and One-Off Costs of Starting University

> **Module:** Financing Education
> **Lesson:** 4 of 10
> **Reading time:** ~5 minutes
> **Audience:** Ages 15–18, international school students

---

## The Invoice That Arrives Before Everything Else

In Lessons 1 through 3, you built a solid picture of what university costs per year — your TCA, the six categories, how figures vary by destination. You now know what the ongoing bill looks like.

This lesson is about a different problem: the bill that arrives before you even start.

Most students discover it the hard way. They have saved carefully for their first year. Then, in the weeks before departure, invoices appear for things they never budgeted for: visa fees, a housing deposit, a laptop that finally gave up, flights, and a pile of household supplies for a flat they have never seen. The money is gone before classes begin, and the regular annual budget has not even started yet.

These are **startup costs**: one-off expenses that land at the very beginning of your university journey, outside the normal TCA rhythm, and before most funding sources have activated.

---

## Why Startup Costs Catch Students Off Guard

Two things make startup costs particularly dangerous.

First, they are invisible in most university cost estimates. University websites publish TCA figures covering tuition and living costs. They do not publish a "things you need to pay before you arrive" list. Students who rely only on official estimates walk into a gap.

Second, the timing is brutal. Student loans typically disburse at the start of term — not before. Scholarships pay into university accounts, not your bank account, and not in advance. Part-time work has not started yet. The startup costs hit at the exact moment when your funding sources are least accessible.

The result: students who planned carefully for their annual TCA sometimes arrive at university having already drained their savings buffer — and the year has not begun.

## The Startup Cost Categories

### Application and Testing Fees

These hit earliest and are easy to forget because they are spread across many months.

US university applications typically cost $60–$90 per school. Applying to ten schools — common for competitive applicants — costs $600–$900 in fees alone. Standardised tests add more: the SAT costs $60, the ACT $93, and IELTS or TOEFL (required by most universities for non-native English speakers) costs $220–$310 per sitting. Many students sit these tests more than once.

UK applications through UCAS cost £28.50 for most applications. EU and Asian universities vary, but international application fees of €50–€100 per school are common.

Budget: $500–$1,500 depending on how many universities you apply to and which tests you need.

### Visa Fees and Administration

Any student studying outside their home country almost certainly needs a visa, and visas cost money.

The UK Student visa application fee is £363 for applications made outside the UK. On top of that, the Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) — which gives you access to the NHS — costs £776 per year of study. For a three-year degree, that is £2,328 in IHS alone, typically paid upfront in full when you apply.

The US F-1 student visa costs $160 for the consulate fee plus a $350 SEVIS (Student and Exchange Visitor Program) fee — $510 total. Some countries require additional health checks or biometric appointments, each with their own fees.

EU students studying within the EU usually face fewer visa requirements, though some non-EU students studying in EU countries need residence permits that carry their own administrative costs.

Budget: £363–£2,700+ for UK; ~$510 for US; varies significantly by destination and passport.

### Housing Deposit and First Month's Rent

This is typically the largest startup cost, and the timing is rigid. Landlords and university housing offices require a deposit — usually one to two months' rent — before you can confirm your room. In most cases, first month's rent is also due at the same time.

On a £700/month flat, that means £700–£1,400 deposit plus £700 first month's rent = £1,400–£2,100 due before you have moved in a single box. Private landlords may also require a guarantor or reference checks with their own administrative fees.

This money is not lost — deposits are typically refunded when you leave if the property is undamaged — but it is tied up for the entire duration of your tenancy and must be available in cash before you can secure housing.

Budget: one to three months' rent, paid upfront. At typical student rents of £500–£900/month, expect £1,000–£2,700.

### Technology

Most universities assume you arrive with a working laptop capable of running their required software. If yours is old, slow, or incompatible, the solution is a new one — and "new laptop" is not a line item in most TCA estimates because it feels like a personal purchase rather than a university cost.

The reality is that most students buy or upgrade a laptop in the months before university, typically spending $600–$1,500. Some programmes (architecture, film, engineering, design) require specific hardware or software that adds further cost.

Check your university's recommended specifications before buying anything. Many universities offer student discounts through Apple, Dell, or Lenovo — sometimes 10–20% off — and some provide free licences for software like Microsoft Office, Adobe Creative Cloud, or MATLAB.

Budget: $0 if your current laptop is adequate; $600–$1,500 for a new one.

### Travel and Moving Costs

Getting yourself — and your belongings — to your university city costs money that does not appear in any TCA calculation.

For domestic students, this might be a train ticket and a few boxes shipped ahead. For international students, it is international flights plus excess baggage or shipping costs for anything that does not fit in a suitcase. Return flights home at Christmas are often booked months in advance — if you factor them into your startup budget rather than your term-time transport budget, the figure is more honest.

Budget: $200–$1,500 depending on distance and how much you are bringing.

### Setting Up a New Home

Almost nobody arrives at university with everything they need for independent living, and almost nobody budgets for what they will need.

A typical first-month household purchase list includes: bedding and pillows, kitchen equipment (pots, pans, cutlery, plates), cleaning supplies, bathroom essentials, a desk lamp, hangers, a door mat, storage solutions, and various small items that seem trivial individually but total $300–$800 collectively.

Students moving into furnished accommodation need less. Students taking unfurnished private flats need significantly more.

Budget: $200–$800 for furnished; $500–$1,500 for unfurnished.

## What the Total Looks Like

Adding these up for a realistic international student scenario:

| Startup cost | Estimate |
|---|---|
| Application and testing fees | $700 |
| Visa fees (UK, 3-year degree) | £2,700 (~$3,400) |
| Housing deposit + first month (£700/month) | £1,400 (~$1,750) |
| Laptop upgrade | $900 |
| Flights and moving costs | $800 |
| Household setup | $500 |
| **Total** | **~$8,050** |

That is roughly $8,000 needed before the first lecture — on top of whatever TCA savings the student has. A student who saved exactly their Year 1 TCA has already overspent before term begins.

The range across different scenarios runs from roughly $3,000 (domestic student, no visa, adequate laptop, furnished room) to over $10,000 (international student, UK visa with full IHS, unfurnished flat, laptop upgrade, long-haul flights).

## How to Plan for Startup Costs

The fix is straightforward once you know the problem exists: treat startup costs as a separate budget, planned separately from TCA, funded separately from your annual living budget.

Three practical steps:

**List your specific startup costs early.** Do not use the generic estimates above as your final numbers — research your exact visa fees, your exact deposit requirement, your actual laptop needs. The real figures are knowable.

**Time your funding.** Identify which of your funding sources will actually be accessible before term starts. Savings are accessible. Loans usually disburse at term start, not before. Scholarship money usually goes to the university, not you. Family contributions depend on the agreement. Map the timing carefully.

**Build a startup buffer separately.** Keep startup cost savings in a separate account from your annual living budget. Students who mix them tend to spend into the startup buffer without realising it, then discover the problem when it is too late to do anything about it.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Startup costs are one-off expenses that arrive before university begins — outside the regular TCA budget
- They typically total $3,000–$10,000 depending on destination, visa requirements, and individual circumstances
- The timing is the real danger: startup costs hit before loans disburse, before scholarships activate, and before any income starts
- Major categories: application/test fees, visa costs, housing deposit, technology, travel, and household setup
- Plan and fund startup costs separately from your annual TCA — they are a different problem with different timing
