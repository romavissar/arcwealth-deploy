## M1-L1: What University Really Costs

**Key term:** Total Cost of Attendance (TCA)
**One-line concept:** University costs six things, not one — and only one of them is on the website.

### Hook
> "Two students. Same university. Same tuition. One pays $10,000 more per year."
> *Why?*

### Concept Cards (5 cards)

**Card 1**
Tuition is the price on the website. It is not the cost of going. The real figure — called **Total Cost of Attendance (TCA)** — includes everything that leaves your account while you are a student.

**Card 2**
Students who plan only for tuition regularly run out of money by their second semester. TCA exists so that does not happen to you.

**Card 3**
TCA has six categories: tuition, accommodation, food, transport, course materials, and personal spending. Together, these are your real annual cost.

**Card 4**
Your behaviour controls some of these categories. Tuition is fixed. Rent can be negotiated. Food can be managed. Personal spending is almost entirely up to you.

**Card 5**
A three-year degree that takes four years does not cost 33% more — it costs 40–50% more once you add a full extra year of living costs. Always plan for this possibility.

## M1-L2: Tuition, Living Costs, and Total Cost of Attendance

**Key term:** Cost categories
**One-line concept:** Each of the six TCA categories behaves differently — knowing which ones you can control is the first step to managing them.

### Hook
> "€600 rent. €300 food. €100 transport. €600 materials. €200 personal. Add tuition: €12,000."
> *Which number is actually within your control?*

### Concept Cards (6 cards)

**Card 1**
Tuition is set by your university and your programme. Engineering typically costs more than humanities at the same institution. International students almost always pay more than domestic students.

**Card 2**
Accommodation is usually your second-biggest cost. On-campus dormitories are convenient. Off-campus shared flats are often cheaper but require deposits, utility bills, and more planning.

**Card 3**
Food costs vary enormously by approach. University meal plans are predictable but expensive per meal. Self-catering is cheaper but requires time. Most students end up doing both — and overspend on the unplanned bits.

**Card 4**
Transport has three layers: daily commuting, holiday travel home, and spontaneous local movement. If you are studying abroad, factor in at least one or two international flights per year.

**Card 5**
Course materials are easy to underestimate. New textbooks can cost $50–$200 each. Always check whether your university provides free software licences before buying anything. Secondhand copies are often available at a fraction of the cost.

**Card 6**
Personal spending is the most underestimated category because no single purchase feels significant. Phone, clothing, toiletries, gym, subscriptions, socialising — budget $100–$300/month as a floor, more in expensive cities.

## M1-L3: Comparing University Costs — EU, UK, US, and East Asia

**Key term:** Destination cost gap
**One-line concept:** The same student studying the same subject can pay five times more or less depending entirely on which country they choose.

### Hook
> "Same degree. Same subject. One student pays $13,000 total per year. Another pays $77,000."
> *What is the only difference between them?*

### Concept Cards (6 cards)

**Card 1**
The answer is destination. US private universities charge $35,000–$65,000 tuition per year for international students. German public universities charge approximately €0–€500 per semester. Same education. Radically different cost.

**Card 2**
The US is the most expensive destination because most top universities are privately funded — they earn their revenue almost entirely from tuition. There is no government cap on what they can charge international students.

**Card 3**
The UK charges international students market-rate fees (£15,000–£38,000/year) rather than the domestic cap of £9,250. The upside: most UK undergraduate degrees are three years, not four, which cuts total costs by 25%.

**Card 4**
EU countries vary widely. Germany, France, and Norway charge near-zero tuition even to international students — their universities are government-funded. The Netherlands and Ireland charge moderate market rates (€8,000–€20,000). Always check by country, not just "EU".

**Card 5**
In East Asia: Japan's national universities charge ~¥536,000/year (~$3,500) — among the cheapest quality universities globally. Singapore's NUS and NTU are internationally competitive and priced accordingly at S$17,000–S$37,000. Hong Kong sits between the two.

**Card 6**
The four-year cumulative gap between a US private university (~$308,000) and a low-fee EU destination (~$52,000) is over $250,000. Two students, same subject, same ambitions — one graduates with a quarter-million dollars more in financial freedom.

## M1-L4: Hidden and One-Off Costs of Starting University

**Key term:** Startup costs
**One-line concept:** Before you attend a single lecture, you may spend $3,000–$10,000 on costs that almost nobody warns you about.

### Hook
> "Priya budgeted perfectly for Year 1. Then the invoice for her visa, deposit, and laptop arrived."
> *She had not budgeted for any of it.*

### Concept Cards (5 cards)

**Card 1**
**Startup costs** are the one-off expenses that hit before or during your first weeks at university. Unlike TCA categories, they do not repeat every year — but they must be paid before you even begin.

**Card 2**
Application fees add up faster than expected. US university applications cost $50–$90 each. Applying to ten schools is $500–$900 before you have been accepted anywhere. Standardised tests (SAT, ACT, IELTS, TOEFL) add another $100–$250 each.

**Card 3**
Visa fees and required health checks apply to most international students. A UK student visa costs £363 plus an Immigration Health Surcharge of £776 per year of study. US student visa (F-1) costs $160 plus the SEVIS fee of $350.

**Card 4**
Housing deposits (typically one to two months' rent) must be paid upfront before you move in. First-month rent is often due simultaneously. Together, these can total $1,500–$3,000 before you have unpacked a single box.

**Card 5**
Technology, bedding, kitchenware, and travel to campus are all startup costs. A necessary laptop upgrade alone can be $500–$1,500. Most students spend $500–$1,500 on household supplies in their first month. Budget for all of it before you arrive.

## M1-L5: Family Support, Cultural Expectations, and the Bank of Mom & Dad

**Key term:** Family funding agreement
**One-line concept:** Family money comes with expectations — and understanding those expectations before you leave is better than discovering them during a phone call in semester two.

### Hook
> "Marcus assumed his parents would cover everything. His parents assumed he would cover everything. Neither had said this out loud."
> *What happens next?*

### Concept Cards (5 cards)

**Card 1**
Family financial support for university varies enormously by culture, family structure, and expectation. In some families, full parental funding is the default. In others, students are expected to be financially independent from age 18. Neither is right — but both require a conversation.

**Card 2**
The **Bank of Mom & Dad** is informal, flexible, and interest-free — but it is not unconditional. Family funding often comes with strings: choice of subject, choice of location, expected grades, expected career paths. Undiscussed strings become conflict.

**Card 3**
A **family funding agreement** does not need to be a legal document. It is a clear conversation — ideally written down — covering: how much will be contributed, when, for how long, and what happens if circumstances change.

**Card 4**
Students who rely on family funding without a formal agreement often discover mid-year that the agreement was different in their heads than in their parents'. The safest plan always includes a scenario where family support reduces or stops.

**Card 5**
Cultural expectations about financial independence differ significantly. In East Asian and South Asian family structures, parental funding through university is often assumed. In many Western countries, students are expected to take loans and work. Neither model is universal — understanding your family's model explicitly is the starting point.

## M1-L6: Scholarships, Grants, and Financial Aid

**Key term:** Merit vs need-based funding
**One-line concept:** Free money for university exists — but it goes to the students who find it, apply early, and stack multiple sources together.

### Hook
> "Two students. Identical qualifications. One pays full price. One pays 40% less."
> *The difference? One applied for scholarships. The other assumed they would not qualify.*

### Concept Cards (6 cards)

**Card 1**
**Scholarships** are awarded based on merit — academic performance, talent, leadership, or community contribution. They do not need to be repaid. A student who does not apply does not receive one.

**Card 2**
**Grants** and **financial aid** are awarded based on financial need. Universities, governments, and NGOs all offer them. US universities in particular use need-based aid to bring their headline price down significantly for eligible families.

**Card 3**
Most scholarships have application deadlines 6–12 months before your programme starts. Applying late is the single most common reason qualified students miss funding. Build a scholarship calendar before you apply to universities.

**Card 4**
Stacking means applying for multiple small scholarships simultaneously. Three awards of $2,000 each are worth the same as one $6,000 scholarship — and the smaller ones attract fewer applicants. Most students stop at one application. The students who fund their degrees apply to ten or more.

**Card 5**
External scholarships — from companies, foundations, charities, and governments — exist separately from university aid. They are less well-known, which makes them less competitive. Your own government may offer study-abroad scholarships you have never heard of. Research your home country's schemes.

**Card 6**
For US universities, the **FAFSA** (Free Application for Federal Student Aid) is the gateway to most need-based aid. International students are ineligible for FAFSA, but many US universities have their own institutional aid for international students — always ask directly.

## M1-L7: Student Loans — How They Work Across Different Regions

**Key term:** Income-contingent repayment
**One-line concept:** Not all student loans work the same way — where you study determines whether your loan is a manageable tool or a lifelong burden.

### Hook
> "Two graduates. Both borrowed $30,000. One repaid it in 5 years. One is still repaying 20 years later."
> *Same amount. Very different loan systems.*

### Concept Cards (7 cards)

**Card 1**
A **student loan** lets you attend university now and repay later. The cost of borrowing is interest — a percentage charged annually on your outstanding balance. Different loan systems charge very different rates and have very different repayment rules.

**Card 2**
In the **US**, federal student loans (available via FAFSA, US citizens only) charge fixed interest rates of 5–8% and offer income-driven repayment plans. Private loans (from banks) carry higher rates and fewer protections. International students in the US typically cannot access federal loans.

**Card 3**
In the **UK**, Student Finance England charges a Plan 5 interest rate (RPI + up to 3%, currently around 7.1%) but uses **income-contingent repayment**: you only repay 9% of income above £25,000/year. If you never earn enough, unpaid loans are written off after 40 years.

**Card 4**
**Income-contingent repayment** means your monthly payment is tied to what you earn, not what you borrowed. A UK graduate earning £28,000 repays just £270/year — regardless of their total loan balance. This protects low earners from unmanageable debt.

**Card 5**
In the **EU**, student loan availability varies dramatically by country. Germany, France, and the Nordics have low or no tuition, so most students do not need large loans. Some EU countries offer state-backed student loans; many do not. Family funding is often the primary model.

**Card 6**
In **East Asia**, formal public student loan systems are limited. Japan's JASSO loan programme is the main exception, with interest-free options for eligible students. In Singapore, China, South Korea, and Hong Kong, family funding is typically the primary model — public loan infrastructure is limited compared to the US and UK.

**Card 7**
The most dangerous borrowing is **private student loans** (especially in the US) and consumer credit used as a substitute for a proper student loan. These carry high interest rates, no income-contingent protections, and can create debt that grows faster than a graduate's ability to repay.

## M1-L8: Part-Time Work, Work-Study, Side Hustles, and Entrepreneurship

**Key term:** Visa work restrictions
**One-line concept:** Earning money at university is possible — but the rules, limits, and trade-offs depend heavily on your visa status and how much your studies actually demand.

### Hook
> "Marcus earns €500/month from a campus job. Kai earns €0 because he assumed his visa didn't allow it."
> *Kai assumed wrong.*

### Concept Cards (6 cards)

**Card 1**
Most student visas permit some paid work — but with limits. UK Tier 4 students can work up to 20 hours/week during term. US F-1 students can work on-campus up to 20 hours/week during term; off-campus work requires special authorisation. EU students studying within the EU typically face fewer restrictions. Always verify your specific visa terms.

**Card 2**
**Work-study programmes** (common in the US) are government-funded arrangements where eligible students work part-time on campus. The jobs are designed around academic schedules and often pay above minimum wage. Availability depends on financial need and is allocated through the FAFSA — international students are typically ineligible.

**Card 3**
Campus jobs — library assistants, lab support, sports coaching, tutoring — are worth prioritising over off-campus work. They are shorter commutes, more flexible around exam periods, and employers understand academic commitments better.

**Card 4**
Freelancing and gig economy work (tutoring, translation, design, copywriting, delivery) offer flexible income but less predictability. Tax obligations on freelance income vary by country — in the UK, earnings above £1,000/year from self-employment must be declared via Self Assessment. In Germany, a Freelancer visa or Gewerbe registration may be required.

**Card 5**
The real cost of part-time work is time. 10 hours/week = 400+ hours/year. Research consistently shows that more than 15–20 hours/week of paid work correlates with lower grades. The financial return must be worth the academic cost.

**Card 6**
Student entrepreneurship — building a small product, service, or online business — can generate income while building skills. However, it carries zero guarantee of income and high time demands. For funding reliability, it is a supplement, not a substitute for a proper funding plan.

## M1-L9: Evaluating the Financial Return on Your Degree

**Key term:** Return on investment (ROI)
**One-line concept:** A degree is a financial investment — and like any investment, the return depends on what you paid and what you earn.

### Hook
> "Two graduates. Both studied for four years. One earns $85,000 in year one. One earns $28,000."
> *Both degrees cost the same. Was that the right price for both?*

### Concept Cards (6 cards)

**Card 1**
**Return on investment (ROI)** applied to education compares what you paid (total TCA over the degree) against what you earn (starting salary and career earnings trajectory). A higher cost degree is not necessarily a worse investment — but it requires a higher return to justify the premium.

**Card 2**
The simplest ROI measure: divide your total degree cost by your expected first-year salary. A $150,000 degree that leads to a $75,000 starting salary has a debt-to-income ratio of 2:1 — generally considered manageable. A $200,000 degree with a $35,000 starting salary has a 5.7:1 ratio — significantly harder to service.

**Card 3**
Programme choice matters as much as institution. Medicine, law, engineering, and computer science consistently produce higher starting salaries than many humanities or arts programmes at the same institution. This is not a reason to avoid any subject — it is a reason to understand the financial trade-off honestly.

**Card 4**
Starting salary is not the whole story. Career trajectory, job satisfaction, professional flexibility, and earning potential at age 35–45 often diverge significantly from age-22 starting points. A teacher starting at $35,000 may earn $75,000 by career peak. A finance graduate starting at $90,000 may plateau or face redundancy.

**Card 5**
Location significantly affects both sides of the ROI equation. A $300,000 US private university degree in computer science, placed in San Francisco, may generate a 20-year career worth $5,000,000+. The same student, same degree, placed in a smaller market, earns significantly less. Destination and industry placement matter.

**Card 6**
ROI is one lens, not the only one. Quality of life, intellectual fulfilment, personal values, and where you want to live are real factors that no salary table captures. The goal is not to optimise purely for income — it is to make a financially conscious decision, not a financially oblivious one.

## M1-L10: Building a Personal University Funding Plan

**Key term:** Funding plan
**One-line concept:** A funding plan is not a wish list — it is a specific, sourced, numbered account of how every year of your degree will be paid for.

### Hook
> "Marcus has a university place. He has a vague sense his parents will help and he might get a scholarship."
> *That is not a plan. That is a hope.*

### Concept Cards (5 cards)

**Card 1**
A **funding plan** is a document — however simple — that lists: your annual TCA, every confirmed source of funding, the gap between them, and specifically how that gap will be closed. Every item should be a confirmed number, not an estimate or a hope.

**Card 2**
The four main funding sources to combine are: family contribution, scholarships and grants, student loans, and earned income. A strong plan does not rely entirely on any single one. Diversification in funding is as important as diversification in a financial portfolio.

**Card 3**
Confirmed vs unconfirmed: family support is unconfirmed until a specific amount and payment schedule has been agreed. A scholarship application is unconfirmed until the award letter arrives. Build your plan on confirmed sources first, then layer in likely but unconfirmed sources.

**Card 4**
Contingency planning means having a written answer to: "What do I do if family support drops by 50%? What if I miss my scholarship? What if my part-time job disappears?" A plan without contingencies is a plan that collapses at the first unexpected event.

**Card 5**
Your funding plan should be rebuilt annually. Year 2 is different from Year 1: startup costs disappear, scholarship applications can be renewed or new ones sought, earned income typically increases as you find better campus roles. Review and update every August before the new academic year.

## M2-L1: Why Budgeting Matters

**Key term:** Intentional spending
**One-line concept:** A budget is not a restriction — it is a tool that makes sure your money goes where you actually want it to go.

### Hook
> "Marcus earned €900 last month. At the end of the month he had €47 left and couldn't explain where the rest went."
> *Sound familiar?*

### Concept Cards (5 cards)

**Card 1**
Most people do not run out of money because they earn too little. They run out because they do not track where money goes — and small, invisible spending accumulates into large, invisible losses.

**Card 2**
A **budget** is a plan for your money before you spend it. Not a record of what you already spent. Not a restriction. A decision made in advance about where your money will go.

**Card 3**
**Intentional spending** means choosing where your money goes based on what actually matters to you. An unbudgeted student does not have more freedom — they have less, because money disappears into low-value spending before reaching high-value goals.

**Card 4**
Budgeting does not require perfection or detailed spreadsheets. The minimum viable budget has three numbers: income this month, essential costs this month, and the gap left for everything else.

**Card 5**
Students who budget are not more restricted than those who do not. They are more likely to have money when it matters — a last-minute flight home, an unexpected textbook, a night out worth remembering.
